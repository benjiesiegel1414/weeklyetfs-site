What "Price Decay" Means on WeeklyETFs.com

Across every list on WeeklyETFs.com, each ETF is tagged with a simple Yes/No flag for Price Decay. That flag answers one specific question: has the fund's share price dropped below where it started at inception? It looks at price only, not at the dividends paid out along the way, which is why we track it as its own column separate from Total Return. Investors sometimes call this "NAV decay," since most of these funds report price relative to net asset value per share, and the two terms point at the same underlying behavior: a shrinking share price over time.

This matters because many weekly high yield ETFs run options-based income strategies — covered calls, synthetic positions, or other derivative overlays — that can produce a large headline yield while still allowing the share price to erode in the background. A fund that's been flagged "No" for Price Decay has, at least so far, avoided that erosion. The list below pulls only those names.

Zero Decay Doesn't Mean Zero Risk

A clean price history is reassuring, but it's a backward-looking snapshot, not a forecast. An ETF can carry a "No" decay flag today and still see its share price slip tomorrow if the underlying holdings sell off, if the options strategy underperforms, or if a large distribution is later reclassified as return of capital. Treat this list as a starting point for research, not a finish line — pair it with the Total Return column above and a look at each fund's own distribution history before drawing conclusions.

Why Some Weekly ETFs Avoid NAV Decay While Others Don't

There's no single reason a fund avoids price decay, but a few patterns show up often among the names that do:

  • Underlying appreciation: if the stock, index, or basket a fund is built on rises enough, it can outpace whatever the options overlay gives up, keeping share price flat or higher.
  • More conservative option strategies: funds that sell calls further out-of-the-money or cover a smaller percentage of the portfolio tend to cap less upside, leaving more room for price growth.
  • Newer launches: a fund that hasn't lived through a full market cycle yet simply hasn't had as much time or opportunity to decay.
  • Distribution policy: some funds pay out closer to what they actually earn rather than a fixed high target, which reduces the odds of paying out more than the strategy generates.

None of these patterns guarantee a fund stays decay-free going forward — they're simply common threads worth understanding before assuming a "No" flag will hold up indefinitely.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does "zero price decay" mean?

It means the ETF's share price has not fallen below its inception price, based on the most recent data in our spreadsheet. It looks at price only, separate from dividends paid.

Is this the same as a positive total return?

Not necessarily. Total Return includes both price change and distributions, so a fund can show zero price decay and still have a different total return figure once dividends are factored in. Check both columns together.

How often does this list change?

It rebuilds every time the page loads, pulling whatever is currently in our spreadsheet. As Price Decay flags are updated or new ETFs are added, names can join or drop off this list.

Why isn't every ETF on WeeklyETFs.com on this list?

This page only shows funds currently flagged "No" for Price Decay. Funds flagged "Yes," or funds without enough data to determine a flag, are left off intentionally. See the full list at WeeklyETFs.com for every tracked ETF.

Important Risk Factors

  • A "No" price decay flag reflects past data only and is not a guarantee against future price declines
  • Distributions may include return of capital, which can erode NAV even when the headline price looks stable
  • Covered call and option-income strategies cap upside potential
  • Single-stock and sector-concentrated ETFs carry added volatility
  • Total return — not the decay flag alone — determines real performance

Always review a fund's official distribution breakdown and consult a licensed financial advisor before investing.
