Why the Highest Yield Isn't Automatically the Best Pick

Ranking by yield alone rewards aggressive options strategies and ignores what's happening to the share price underneath. A fund near the top of this list can still post a disappointing total return if price decay outpaces the income collected, especially in choppy or declining markets for the underlying stock. Before chasing a number at the top of this page, check the Price Decay and Total Return columns next to it — they tell the rest of the story that yield alone leaves out.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is this list ranked?

Every ETF in our database is sorted by current dividend yield, highest to lowest, and the top 25 results are displayed here.

How often does the ranking change?

The list re-sorts every time the page loads, pulling the latest figures from our spreadsheet. Rankings can shift daily as yields are updated or new ETFs are added.

What does "Price Decay" mean?

It flags whether an ETF's share price has fallen since inception — common among option-income strategies that distribute large yields partly through return of capital.

Are these the same names as the homepage list?

They're drawn from the same database. This page simply isolates the 25 highest current yields rather than showing the full 100+ name list.

Important Risk Factors

  • High yields are not guaranteed and can change quickly
