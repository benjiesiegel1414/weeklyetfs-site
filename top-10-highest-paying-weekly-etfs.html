If you're hunting for the absolute highest weekly dividend yields on the market, this list ranks the 10 highest-paying weekly dividend ETFs currently tracked in our database, by current distribution yield. Nearly every fund on this list is a single-stock or theme-based options-income ETF — meaning the eye-catching yield comes from selling options premium against an underlying stock or index, not from traditional corporate dividends.

That distinction matters enormously for your decision-making, so before you scroll the rankings, read the risk note below.

⚠️ Why these yields are so high (and what that means for you): Most ETFs on this list use a synthetic covered-call / options-income strategy on a single volatile stock (like a leveraged crypto miner, meme stock, or high-beta tech name). These strategies generate large weekly or monthly cash distributions by selling options premium, but that premium is frequently funded by NAV (share price) erosion over time — which is why almost every fund below shows "Price Decay: YES." A 100%+ yield does not mean you are earning 100%+ in real wealth growth. Always check Total Return (not just yield) before assuming a fund is "winning."

Frequently Asked Questions

Are these the safest weekly dividend ETFs?

No — quite the opposite. The highest-yielding weekly dividend ETFs are almost always the highest-risk ones. These funds use leveraged options strategies on single, volatile stocks, and most have experienced significant price decay. If you're looking for steadier, lower-risk weekly income, check our Best Weekly Dividend ETF guide instead, which weighs consistency alongside yield.

Why is the yield so much higher than the total return?

Because dividend yield only measures income paid out — it says nothing about what happens to your share price. Options-income ETFs often distribute a large portion of the underlying stock's option premium as "dividends," which can come at the cost of NAV erosion over time. Total return (price change + dividends) gives the fuller picture of whether you actually made money.

What does "Price Decay: Yes" mean?

It means the ETF's share price has fallen since the fund's inception date. It's a useful early-warning flag, though it doesn't capture the dividends collected along the way — that's why we also show Total Return for full context.

How often do these ETFs actually pay?

Most funds on this list pay weekly or monthly distributions, depending on the issuer (Roundhill's WeeklyPay series pays weekly; many YieldMax and GraniteShares funds pay monthly despite being grouped in the broader "weekly income ETF" category by income investors). Always confirm the exact distribution schedule on the issuer's official fund page before investing.

Should I just buy the highest-yielding ETF on this list?

Not necessarily. This is purely a yield ranking, not a recommendation. High yield + high price decay can mean you're recycling your own capital back to yourself rather than earning incremental income. Always weigh yield against total return, your risk tolerance, and your overall portfolio goals — and consult a licensed financial advisor before investing.
